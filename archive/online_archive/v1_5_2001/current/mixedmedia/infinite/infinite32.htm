In The Accursed Mazurka there is probably no one shot longer than 15 seconds. Rarely is there a single image. Bottles of prescription drugs, a woman, a hospital building, trees, all merging but somewhat distinct on the screen. Frames are superimposed once, twice, three times. Images from movies are shot off the television so that a vertical roll prohibits a clear reading. Images of writing, writing we can only glimpse at, pervade the film. Here is a language. I am mute.38 Letters make patterns, words are written in shadows, a splice mark in the middle of a frame is a form of punctuation. Water drops blurring the marks of a felt tip pen are played in reverse and for a moment one imagines that one will be able to read the words being unveiled. A new language is being formed. One that merges, without privilege, text, sound, and image - a language of duration-an essay which is forty minutes long. Each shot is describable in itself, but the language formed is one of connections and of experience. She cannot occupy her body through her senses, the only way which interests her - her reality - the basis of every other reality - life itself. Yet life continues.39 The film gives an experience to the viewer, not by relating it, but by making it.

What I conceive is writing as an actual creation. It is the birth of another cycle. 40

On the computer the sound of the typing of italic, plain, and bold text is the same. The difference only in the rhythm, speed, and pressure with which each individual typist strikes the keys. The page is a map and through visual markings a reader can hear her own audio track. Not only the sounds of the words but the sounds of the image of the words. Italics are unanchored, floating, possibly the sound of a light rain. Plain text is the typewriter striking the paper in even tempo. The bold text adds an echo; resonating with its own imprint.
