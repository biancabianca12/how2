Nina Fonoroff''s beautifully fragmented film The Accursed Mazurka chronicles a woman's mental illness. Her own experience of this illness. The subject, now distanced, now clinical, is herself. Bits of images, bits of sound, remembrances personal and cultural (she ignores the copyright - doesn't Rogers and Hammerstein belong to her generation) woven and layered upon each other to make a whole. (That is if one accounts for the hole in a whole.) A woman opens and closes a set of venetian blinds. The patterned light falls into the room and is shut out. Different voices read the medical logs of a patient separated from her body, a body swimming in a depression. She feels distanced from her own body an observer unable to intervene. A biographer? An artists work is sometimes referred to as her body of work. The separation of Fonoroff from her film is one of the elements of the films success. No victimization here, no blame, no confessions, just the richness of an experience, that, as the voice over remarks, was a curse or perhaps a blessing.

I suspect good health is only an elaborate device. An enormous apparatus we carry with us at all times like an exo-skeleton. Some of us, for a time, are cursed or privileged to feel this device dropping away with an unimaginable violence. We feel every nerve ending in us is live. Never before and never again will we be so alive and so dead that our every instinct is to cry out for the apparatus to be restored to us. 6

Optical printing allows for the mixing, reframing, general alteration of the image. A moment captured is no necessary truth. Peeling, layering, one image on top of another, in the negative, upside down, sometimes this is closer to the experience of the mind. The Doctor writes for the experience of the mind. To distill the imagination. To explode words. This is different from psychoanalysis. Fonoroff's film ends on the note of not allocating blame, of not looking to the family for the answers, of not singing to the narrative of psychoanalysis. Development continues, it does not stop at childhood. Experience is the richness of the blends of images and sounds which make up our environments, an experience that at times our body must withdraw from.
